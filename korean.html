Learning Korean

Noun Particle

In the Korean language, noun particles play a crucial role in identifying the function of nouns within a sentence.

Here’s a summary of some important noun particles, along with simple examples:

  1. 은/는 (Topic marker): Used to mark the topic of a sentence, which may or may not be the grammatical subject.

    • 나는 학생이에요. (As for me, I’m a student.)

  2. 이/가 (Subject marker): Used to mark the grammatical subject of a sentence.

    • 강아지가 귀여워요. (The puppy is cute.)

  3. 을/를 (Object marker): Used to mark the direct object of a verb.

    • 제가 케이크를 먹어요. (I eat the cake.)

  4. 에게/한테 (Indirect object marker): Indicates the recipient or target of an action.

    • 선생님께 질문을 했어요. (I asked a question to the teacher.)

  5. 에/에서 (Location marker): ‘에’ indicates the destination or location, while ‘에서’ indicates the origin or where an action takes place.

    • 집에 가요. (I’m going home.)
    • 학교에서 공부해요. (I study at school.)

  6. 와/과 (Noun connector): Used to link nouns together, similar to “and” in English.

    • 커피와 차를 좋아해요. (I like coffee and tea.)

  7. 도 (Additive particle): Adds emphasis or indicates “also” or “too.”

    • 나도 영화를 보러 가고 싶어요. (I also want to go watch a movie.)

  8. 만 (Limiting particle): Used to show exclusivity or “only.”

    • 오직 친구만 믿어요. (I trust only my friends.)

  9. 의 (Possessive particle): Indicates possession or association, similar to “of” or the apostrophe-s in English.

    • 나의 엄마는 요리사예요. (My mom is a chef.)

  10. 로/으로 (Direction or method): Indicates direction, purpose, or a method used to achieve something.

  • 학교로 가는 길이에요. (It’s the way to school.)
  • 포크로 음식을 먹어요. (I eat food with a fork.)
  1. 부터 (From, since): Marks the starting point of an action, time, or range.
  • 아침 9시부터 오후 5시까지 일해요. (I work from 9 a.m. to 5 p.m.)
  1. 까지 (Until, up to, to): Marks the endpoint of an action, time, or range.
  • 지금부터 저녁까지 여기에 있어요. (I’ll be here from now until evening.)
  1. 같이/함께 (Together, with): Indicates doing something together with someone or something.
  • 친구와 함께 놀러 가요. (I go to play with my friend.)
  1. 대신 (Instead of): Used to indicate that something is done in place of something else.
  • 커피 대신 차를 마셔요. (I drink tea instead of coffee.)
  1. 보다 (Compared to, more than): Used for comparisons.
  • 날씨가 어제보다 더워요. (The weather is hotter than yesterday.)
  1. 마다 (Every, each): Used to express regular intervals or recurrence.
  • 주말마다 산책을 해요. (I go for a walk every weekend.)
  1. 전 (Before, ago): Indicates a time before a specific event or point in time.
  • 일주일 전에 여행을 갔어요. (I went on a trip a week ago.)
  1. 후 (After): Indicates a time after a specific event or point in time.
  • 회의 후에 점심을 먹을 거예요. (I’ll eat lunch after the meeting.)
  1. 처럼 (Like, as): Used to make comparisons or indicate similarity.
  • 그녀는 천사처럼 예뻐요. (She is pretty like an angel.)
  1. 아래/위 (Under, below/above, over): Used to describe the position or rank of something.
  • 책상 위에 책이 있어요. (There’s a book on the desk.)
  • 나이 18세 아래는 입장이 금지예요. (Entry is prohibited for those under 18 years old.)
  1. 덕분에 (Thanks to): Used to show gratitude or appreciation for someone or something responsible for a positive outcome.
  • 친구 덕분에 시험에 합격했어요. (Thanks to my friend, I passed the exam.)
  1. 때문에 (Because of, due to): Used to indicate the cause or reason for something.
  • 비가 와서 때문에 우산을 가져가요. (I take an umbrella because it’s raining.)
  1. 쯤 (About, around, approximately): Used to give an approximate value or amount.
  • 여기까지 오는 데 한 시간쯤 걸렸어요. (It took about an hour to get here.)
  1. 밖에 (Only, nothing but): Used to emphasize a small amount or the exclusivity of something.
  • 남은 건 돈 5000원 밖에 없어요. (All I have left is 5,000 won.)
  1. (으)로서 (As, in the capacity of): Indicates one’s role or position in a certain context.
  • 선생님으로서 학생들을 가르쳐야 해요. (As a teacher, I have to teach the students.)
  1. 없이 (Without): Used to express the absence of something.
  • 너 없이는 살 수 없어요. (I can’t live without you.)
  1. 통해/을 통해 (Through, via): Indicates a medium or method used to achieve something.
  • 인터넷을 통해 쇼핑을 해요. (I shop through the internet.)
  1. (이)나 (Or, either): Used to offer alternatives or choices between two or more items.
  • 커피나 차를 마실래요? (Do you want to drink coffee or tea?)
  1. 뿐만 아니라 (Not only… but also): Used to add additional information or emphasize the presence of something else.
  • 그녀는 똑똑할 뿐만 아니라 아름답기도 해요. (She is not only smart but also beautiful.)
  1. 처음에/마지막에 (At first, in the beginning/at last, in the end): Indicates the starting point or endpoint of a series of events or actions.
  • 처음에는 어려웠지만, 결국에는 성공했어요. (It was difficult at first, but in the end, I succeeded.)
  1. 무렵 (Around, about): Used to indicate an approximate time or age.
  • 저녁 6시 무렵에 도착할 거예요. (I will arrive around 6 p.m.)
  1. 당시 (At that time, then): Refers to a specific time or period in the past.
  • 학창 시절 당시에는 운동을 잘 했어요. (I was good at sports back in my school days.)
  1. 뒤/앞 (Behind, after/ in front of, before): Used to describe the position, location, or time sequence of something.
  • 나는 그녀 뒤에 서 있어요. (I am standing behind her.)
  • 국어 시험 앞에 수학 시험 (A math exam before a language exam)
  1. 걸쳐 (Across, over, throughout): Indicates the range or extent of an action or event.
  • 3개월 걸쳐 프로젝트를 완료했어요. (I completed the project over three months.)
  1. 대로 (As, according to): Used to express conformity or compliance with a rule or instruction.
  • 엄마가 말한 대로 했어요. (I did as my mom told me.)
  1. 옆에/곁에 (Next to, beside): Used to describe the position or location of something.
  • 나는 친구 옆에 앉아요. (I sit next to my friend.)
  1. 덕에 (Thanks to): Similar to 덕분에, used to show gratitude or appreciation for someone or something responsible for a positive outcome.
  • 선생님 덕에 한국어를 배웠어요. (Thanks to my teacher, I learned Korean.)
  1. 속에/안에 (Inside, within): Used to describe the position or location of something.
  • 가방 속에 지갑이 있어요. (There’s a wallet inside the bag.)
  1. 중에서 (Among, between): Used to indicate a choice or preference among a group of items or people.
  • 이 영화 중에서 제일 좋아해요. (This is my favorite movie among these.)
  1. 앞으로 (From now on, in the future): Indicates a future action or event.
  • 앞으로 더 열심히 할 거예요. (I will work harder from now on.)

Adjective

Adjectives in Korean are an essential part of the language, used to describe or modify nouns.

Here’s a summary of the key points related to Korean adjectives:

  1. Adjective types: Korean adjectives can be divided into two main categories - descriptive and determinative. Descriptive adjectives describe the qualities of a noun (e.g., 큰 “big”, 작은 “small”), while determinative adjectives indicate a specific noun (e.g., 이/그/저 “this/that/that over there”).

  2. Adjective endings: Korean adjectives typically end in -ㄴ, -는, or -은 when used to modify a noun. For example, 작은 (small) 책 (book) becomes 작은 책 (small book).

  3. Adjective conjugation: Unlike English, Korean adjectives can be conjugated to reflect tense, politeness level, and the speaker’s relationship to the listener. When used in a sentence, adjectives are conjugated similarly to verbs.

    • Present tense: 크다 (to be big) becomes 큰 (big) when modifying a noun and 크다/커요/큽니다 (is big) when used as a predicate.
    • Past tense: 크다 (to be big) becomes 컸다/컸어요/컸습니다 (was big) when used as a predicate.
    • Future tense: 크다 (to be big) becomes 클 거예요/클 것입니다 (will be big) when used as a predicate.

  4. Comparatives and superlatives: In Korean, adjectives don’t have specific comparative or superlative forms like in English. Instead, comparative and superlative meanings are expressed using other words or particles.

    • Comparative: 더 (more) or 보다 (than) are used for comparisons, e.g., 이 책은 저 책보다 더 큰 책이에요 (This book is bigger than that book).
    • Superlative: 가장 (the most) is used to indicate the superlative form of an adjective, e.g., 가장 큰 책 (the biggest book).

  5. Negation: To negate an adjective, the adverb 안 (not) is used before the adjective, e.g., 안 큰 책 (not a big book).

  6. Using adjectives as adverbs: In Korean, adjectives can also function as adverbs without any additional changes. To use an adjective as an adverb, simply place it before a verb.

    • Example: 빨리 걷다 (to walk quickly) - 빨리 (quickly) is the adverb form of the adjective 빠르다 (to be fast).

  7. Compound adjectives: Korean adjectives can be combined to form compound adjectives, which describe a noun with two or more characteristics.

    • Example: 작고 귀여운 강아지 (a small and cute puppy) - 작다 (small) and 귀엽다 (cute) are combined to describe the puppy.

  8. Adjective nominalization: Adjectives can be turned into nouns by attaching the nominalization suffix -ㅁ or -음.

    • Example: 높다 (to be high) becomes 높음 (height); 넓다 (to be wide) becomes 넓음 (width).

  9. Adjective intensifiers: Certain adverbs can be used to intensify the meaning of an adjective, similar to “very” or “extremely” in English.

    • Example: 아주 예쁘다 (very pretty), 매우 빠르다 (very fast), 너무 크다 (too big).

  10. Adjective linking: To link two or more adjectives in a sentence, the conjunction -고 can be attached to the stem of the first adjective.

    • Example: 작고 빨간 자동차 (a small and red car) - 작다 (small) and 빨갛다 (red) are linked by -고.

  11. Adjective reduplication: Some adjectives can be reduplicated to convey emphasis or repetition.

    • Example: 조용조용하다 (very quiet, hushed) - 조용하다 (quiet) is reduplicated for emphasis.

  12. Adjective opposites: Some Korean adjectives have opposite meanings, which can be useful to know for expanding your vocabulary and understanding context.

  • Example: 길다 (long) and 짧다 (short); 높다 (high) and 낮다 (low); 덥다 (hot) and 춥다 (cold).
  1. Adjective derivation: New adjectives can be derived from existing adjectives or nouns by adding the suffix -적, -스럽다, or -답다.
  • Example: 안전하다 (safe) can be derived from 안전 (safety) + -하다; 아름답다 (beautiful) can be derived from 아름 (beauty) + -답다.

  1. Adjective agreement: In Korean, adjectives do not change their form to agree with the noun they modify in terms of gender, number, or case. This simplifies adjective usage compared to some other languages.

  2. Irregular adjectives: Some Korean adjectives have irregular conjugation patterns, which may require memorization and practice to master.

  • Example: 좋다 (to be good) has an irregular conjugation pattern, becoming 좋은 when modifying a noun and 좋아요/좋습니다 as a predicate.
  1. Honorific adjectives: In formal contexts or when speaking to someone of higher status, certain honorific adjectives can be used to show respect.
  • Example: 귀하다 (honorable) and 훌륭하다 (excellent) are examples of honorific adjectives.
  1. Adjective order: In Korean, there is no strict order for multiple adjectives modifying a noun. Instead, the most important or prominent adjective is usually placed closest to the noun.
  • Example: 작은 빨간 자동차 (small red car) or 빨간 작은 자동차 (red small car) - both orders are acceptable.
  1. Adjective negation with 못: In some cases, the adverb 못 (cannot) can be used before the adjective to express inability or incapability.
  • Example: 못 먹는 음식 (food that cannot be eaten) - 못 is used to negate 먹다 (to eat).
  1. Adjective usage in idiomatic expressions: Korean has numerous idioms and expressions that use adjectives in unique ways, often with meanings that differ from their literal translation.
  • Example: 말이 없다 (to have no words) is an idiomatic expression meaning to be speechless or amazed, even though 말 (words) is a noun and 없다 (to not have) is an adjective.

Number system

Korean has two number systems: Sino-number (Chinese) and Native-number

Here’s a summary of when to use native Korean numbers (하나, 둘,셋,넷,다섯,여섯,일곱,여덟,아홉,열, 열하나…) and Sino-Korean numbers (일,이,삼,사,오,육,칠,팔,구,십) in different contexts:

Native Korean numbers:

  1. Counting objects: Use native Korean numbers with counters (개, 명, 마리, etc.) for counting objects, people, or animals.
    • 세 개 (three objects), 두 명 (two people), 네 마리 (four animals)
  2. Age: Native numbers are used to express age, followed by the counter 살.
    • 스물 세 살 (23 years old), 열 네 살 (14 years old)
  3. Hours: When telling time, use native numbers for the hour, followed by the counter 시.
    • 세 시 (three o’clock), 열 시 (ten o’clock)
  4. Ordinal numbers: Use native numbers with the prefix 제, followed by the counter 번째, to indicate order or rank.
    • 제 일 번째 (first), 제 다섯 번째 (fifth)

Sino-Korean numbers:

  1. Dates: Use Sino-Korean numbers for days and months, followed by the counters 일 (day) and 월 (month).
    • 삼 월 이십오 일 (March 25th), 팔 월 구 일 (August 9th)
  2. Years: Use Sino-Korean numbers to express years, followed by the counter 년.
    • 이천이십 년 (2020), 구천구십구 년 (1999)
  3. Minutes: When telling time, use Sino-Korean numbers for minutes, followed by the counter 분.
    • 이십오 분 (25 minutes), 십 분 (10 minutes)
  4. Money: Sino-Korean numbers are used to express amounts of money (won).
    • 오천 원 (5,000 won), 이만 원 (20,000 won)
  5. Percentages: Use Sino-Korean numbers to express percentages, followed by the counter 퍼센트.
    • 오십 퍼센트 (50 percent), 십오 퍼센트 (15 percent)

General rule:
Use native Korean numbers for counting (up to 99), age, and hours.
Use Sino-Korean numbers for dates, years, minutes, money, and percentages.

Slang

  1. 꿀잼 (kkuljaem) - Meaning “honey fun,” this slang is used to describe something that’s really fun or enjoyable. It’s a combination of the word 꿀 (honey) and 잼 (short for 재미, meaning fun or interesting). Usage: 이 영화 진짜 꿀잼이야! (This movie is really fun!)

  2. 노잼 (nojaem) - The opposite of 꿀잼, 노잼 means “no fun” or boring. It’s a combination of the word 노 (no) and 잼 (fun). Usage: 이 파티 노잼이다. (This party is boring.)

  3. 워라벨 (work-life balance) - This abbreviation stands for “work-life balance,” referring to the balance between one’s work and personal life. It’s a trendy term among young professionals in Korea. Usage: 요즘 회사에서 워라벨 실천하려고 노력 중이야. (I’m trying to practice work-life balance at my company these days.)

  4. 남사친 (namsachin) - A guy who is “just a friend.” 남 (nam) is short for 남자 (namja, meaning “man”), 사 (Sae) is short for 사람 (saram, meaning “human”).

  5. 행쇼 (haengsyo) - Let’s be happy. Short for 행복하십시오 (haengbokasipsio), this Korean slang phrase just means “let’s be happy!”.

  6. 대박 (Dae-Bak) - Awesome. You can use this phrase to describe anything and everything from your food, your mood or any recent events that impact you.

  7. 심쿵 (Sim-Kung) - Heart skips a beat / Heartthrob. This is a shortened word which is abbreviated from 심장이 쿵쿵 (Simjangi Kungkung).

  8. 빡친다 (Bbak-Chin-Da) - To be angry or annoyed. Usage: 나 빡쳤어 (I am angry)

  9. 엄친아 / 엄친딸 (Eom-Chin-Ah / Eom-Chin-Ttal) - Mom’s friend’s son/daughter. This refers to someone who seems to have everything going for them.

  10. 썸 (Sseom) - Some. This refers to the stage in a relationship where two people are more than friends but not quite dating yet. Usage: 우리 썸 탈래? (Wanna be in some?)

  11. 갑분싸 (gahb-bun-ssa) - An abbreviation for “갑자기 분위기 싸해진다”. It means “sudden and awkward silence”. Usage: 너가 아재 개그 때문에 분위기 갑분싸 (Becasue of your dad jokes, sudden silence)
